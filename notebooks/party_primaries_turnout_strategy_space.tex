    \section{How Do Parties, Primaries, Turnout, and Strategy
Interact?}\label{how-do-parties-primaries-turnout-and-strategy-interact}

This notebook studies how election outcomes change when several
politically meaningful ingredients move at the same time: party sorting,
primary rules, turnout, strategic voting, group size, candidate supply,
and the general-election system.

The underlying comparison space is

\texttt{electorate\ ×\ primaries\ ×\ turnout\ ×\ strategy\ ×\ majority:minority\ ratio\ ×\ candidate\ slate\ ×\ system}

but the notebook does not run the full discrete cross-product. Instead
it uses a \textbf{mixed Latin hypercube design}. That means the notebook
spreads sampled cases evenly over the continuous parts of the setup,
while still drawing from the discrete institutional choices like the
primary pipeline, turnout profile, strategy profile, and candidate
slate. Later, it uses simple regression summaries to ask which
ingredients are most associated with changes in welfare.

Plurality stays in the notebook throughout as the baseline. That is
important here. The point is not just to compare systems to one another
in the abstract, but to see when they do better or worse than the most
familiar baseline under the same sampled political setting.

\subsection{Preliminary refresher}\label{preliminary-refresher}

The notebook uses the same spatial interpretation as the rest of the
repo. Voters, candidates, and party anchors all live in the same
two-dimensional map. Closer points mean greater ideological similarity.

There are three layers to keep in mind:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  voters and candidates are attached to parties by ideological
  proximity,
\item
  the primary pipeline may remove candidates before the general
  election,
\item
  and the final electoral system chooses an outcome from the candidates
  who remain.
\end{itemize}

If the final winning position is \(y\) and voter \(i\) is at \(x_i\),
then this notebook defines welfare as negative mean distance to the
winner. For the full electorate,

\[
W_{\mathrm{agg}} = - \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \lVert x_i - y \rVert_2.
\]

The same idea is used for the majority and minority groups separately:

\[
W_{\mathrm{maj}} = - \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{maj}}} \sum_{i \in \mathrm{maj}} \lVert x_i - y \rVert_2,
\]

\[
W_{\mathrm{min}} = - \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{min}}} \sum_{i \in \mathrm{min}} \lVert x_i - y \rVert_2.
\]

Because welfare is negative distance, values closer to zero are better.
The notebook also tracks distance from the electorate geometric median.
If the winner is \(y\) and the electorate median is \(m\), then

\[
d_{\mathrm{median}} = \lVert y - m \rVert_2.
\]

The main comparison with primaries is a matched no-primary baseline. Let
\(Z\) collect all the other ingredients in a sampled case: electorate
family, turnout profile, strategy profile, majority share, candidate
slate, and the intensity parameters. For any metric \(M\), system \(s\),
and primary pipeline \(p\), define

\[
\Delta_M(s, p) = \mathbb{E}_{Z}\left[M(s, p, Z) - M(s, \text{No primary}, Z)\right].
\]

So the object of interest is not just the raw performance of a method
with a primary. It is the average change caused by introducing that
primary, after marginalizing over the rest of the sampled political
setting. In the notebook, this expectation is estimated by averaging the
matched within-case differences over the sampled design.

Because welfare is defined as negative distance, the signs of the
welfare deltas are easy to read. A positive \(\Delta_M(s, p)\) means the
primary pipeline improved that metric relative to the matched no-primary
baseline. A negative value means the pipeline made it worse. Later, when
systems are compared directly to plurality, the notebook uses the same
kind of matched-within-case comparison again, but with plurality as the
reference point instead of no primary.

\subsection{The sampled design}\label{the-sampled-design}

The sampled cases vary the same core ingredients as the full conceptual
space, but they do it with a mixed Latin hypercube instead of a
brute-force grid. The continuous pieces are:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  majority share,
\item
  turnout intensity,
\item
  strategy intensity.
\end{itemize}

The sampled categorical pieces are:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  electorate family,
\item
  primary pipeline,
\item
  turnout profile,
\item
  strategy profile,
\item
  candidate slate.
\end{itemize}

A Latin hypercube design starts from the unit cube. If there are \(d\)
continuous variables and the notebook wants \(N\) sampled cases, then
each axis is divided into \(N\) equally sized intervals. For continuous
coordinate \(j\), those intervals are

\[
I_{k,j} = \left[\frac{k-1}{N}, \frac{k}{N}\right), \qquad k = 1, \dots, N.
\]

The design then chooses one sampled point from each interval on each
axis, and permutes those interval choices across dimensions. That gives
a matrix of sampled points

\[
u_i = (u_{i1}, \dots, u_{id}) \in [0,1]^d, \qquad i = 1, \dots, N,
\]

with the key property that every marginal coordinate covers the full
range instead of bunching up in just one corner. In plain language, each
continuous variable gets spread out deliberately, while the joint design
still mixes those variables together.

This notebook uses a mixed version of that idea. The three continuous
coordinates are mapped from \([0,1]\) into meaningful parameter ranges
for majority share, turnout intensity, and strategy intensity. Separate
sampled coordinates are also binned into named categories for electorate
family, primary pipeline, turnout profile, strategy profile, and
candidate slate. Each sampled case is then evaluated under all five
electoral systems, so plurality can still serve as a within-case
baseline.

This is useful because the full cross-product would explode quickly,
while simple random sampling can leave large holes. A mixed Latin
hypercube is a middle ground: it does not evaluate every possible
combination, but it covers the space much more evenly than a naive
random draw.

\subsection{What changes once primaries are
introduced}\label{what-changes-once-primaries-are-introduced}

The next table averages by \texttt{primary\ ×\ system}. Formally, it is
summarizing estimates of the matched-baseline quantity

\[
\Delta_M(s, p) = \mathbb{E}_{Z}\left[M(s, p, Z) - M(s, \text{No primary}, Z)\right],
\]

for different choices of metric \(M\). So each cell is answering a
question like: after averaging over the rest of the sampled political

\subsection{How regressions like this are used in social
science}\label{how-regressions-like-this-are-used-in-social-science}

The summary tables above are good for broad comparisons, but they do not
tell us which ingredients matter most once several political forces move
together. In social science, a common next step is to fit a regression
model that summarizes average associations while adjusting for the other
observed variables in the dataset.

In the most general linear form used here, observation \(i\) is written
as

\[
Y_i = \alpha + \sum_g X_{ig} \beta_g + \sum_h Z_{ih} \gamma_h + \varepsilon_i,
\]

where the \(X\) terms are main effects, transformed continuous
covariates, and the \(Z\) terms are selected interaction blocks. The
model is still descriptive rather than causal, but it is less
restrictive than the earlier purely additive version because it allows
some effects to depend on context.

The notebook uses three continuous quantities in a richer way than
before:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  minority share,
\item
  turnout intensity,
\item
  strategy intensity.
\end{itemize}

Each one is centered, scaled, and entered both linearly and
quadratically. So the model is allowed to represent curved relationships
such as ``harm rises sharply once the minority becomes very small''
instead of forcing everything onto a straight line.

It also adds a small set of politically motivated interaction terms
rather than every possible pairwise combination. The interactions
included here are:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  electorate \(\times\) primary,
\item
  electorate \(\times\) system,
\item
  primary \(\times\) system,
\item
  turnout profile \(\times\) primary,
\item
  turnout profile \(\times\) system,
\item
  strategy profile \(\times\) system,
\item
  minority share \(\times\) primary,
\item
  minority share \(\times\) system,
\item
  turnout intensity \(\times\) turnout profile,
\item
  strategy intensity \(\times\) strategy profile.
\end{itemize}

The notebook looks at four outcomes:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  overall welfare, \(W_{\mathrm{agg}}\),
\item
  majority welfare, \(W_{\mathrm{maj}}\),
\item
  minority welfare, \(W_{\mathrm{min}}\),
\item
  the welfare gap, \(W_{\mathrm{maj}} - W_{\mathrm{min}}\).
\end{itemize}

To summarize importance, the notebook still asks how much fit falls when
one term family is removed and the model is refit:

\[
I_g = R^2_{\mathrm{full}} - R^2_{-g}.
\]

A larger \(I_g\) means that term family is doing more explanatory work
inside this richer model. For the heatmap below, those drops are
normalized within each outcome,

\[
S_g = \frac{I_g}{\sum_h I_h},
\]

so each column can be read as a relative-importance decomposition of the
explained signal for that outcome. That makes it easier to compare
patterns across the four metrics at once.

    \subsubsection{Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

A few broad patterns stand out once the notebook is averaged over
\texttt{1000} sampled cases. First, primaries are not just a neutral
first step. Relative to each system's own matched no-primary baseline,
almost every primary pipeline reduces overall welfare on average, and
minority welfare usually falls even more. The broad political message is
simple: filtering the field before the general election often does real
damage, even when the final election rule stays the same.

Second, the kind of primary matters more than the final election rule in
many of these comparisons. \texttt{Top-4\ open} is clearly the least
damaging pipeline in the notebook. Its aggregate and minority-welfare
deltas stay much closer to zero than the closed pipelines, and for
minority welfare it is the only pipeline that occasionally turns
positive on average, as in \texttt{Top-4\ open\ +\ Condorcet} and
\texttt{Top-4\ open\ +\ IRV}. By contrast, the closed pipelines are much
harsher, especially for minority voters. The worst minority average in
the notebook comes from \texttt{Closed\ plurality\ +\ Score}, and
several other closed combinations are not far behind. For a lay reader,
that points to an intuitive lesson: broadening who reaches the general
election can matter more than changing how the final round is counted.

Third, different comparisons reward different systems. If the question
is which rule keeps the most value relative to its own no-primary
baseline, \texttt{IRV} looks surprisingly resilient on aggregate
welfare, with the smallest average aggregate loss among the five
systems. But if the question is which rule outperforms plurality inside
the same sampled setting, \texttt{Score} is the clearest winner: it has
the best average distance-to-median improvement, the best
aggregate-welfare gain over plurality, and the largest minority-welfare
gain over plurality. \texttt{Approval} also modestly improves on
plurality, while \texttt{IRV} actually does worse than plurality on
average distance and aggregate welfare in that direct comparison. So the
notebook does not end with one universal champion. It ends with a more
interesting point: whether a system looks good depends on what baseline
is being used.

Finally, the regression summaries make the broader pattern hard to miss.
For all four outcomes, the electorate family is by far the strongest
explanatory feature. After that, turnout profile and the primary
pipeline matter much more than the final system label. In other words,
these simulations suggest that arguments about voting rules are only
part of the story. Who the voters are, how the electorate is structured,
and how parties and primaries narrow the field appear to matter even
more for whether majorities and minorities are well served.
