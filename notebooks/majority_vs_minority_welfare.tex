    \section{When Do Electoral Systems Serve Minorities
Well?}\label{when-do-electoral-systems-serve-minorities-well}

Electoral reform debates tend to focus on one question: which voting
rule is best? Should we use ranked-choice voting, approval voting, score
voting, or something else? That is a reasonable question, but it may not
be the most important one.

This notebook takes a step back and asks something broader: when several
things change at once --- who the voters are, how parties select their
nominees, who shows up to vote, and how strategically people behave ---
which of those things actually drives whether an election produces good
outcomes for everyone, including smaller groups that are outnumbered by
a larger majority?

To do that, the notebook varies seven ingredients simultaneously:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  the \textbf{electorate} --- how voters are spatially distributed
  across the ideological map
\item
  the \textbf{primary pipeline} --- whether and how parties filter
  candidates before the general election
\item
  the \textbf{turnout profile} --- which kinds of voters show up and how
  strongly
\item
  the \textbf{strategy profile} --- how much voters deviate from their
  sincere preferences
\item
  the \textbf{majority-to-minority ratio} --- how large or small the
  minority group is
\item
  the \textbf{candidate slate} --- what choices voters are actually
  offered
\item
  the \textbf{electoral system} --- the voting rule used in the general
  election
\end{itemize}

Running every possible combination of these would be computationally
prohibitive, so the notebook uses a \textbf{mixed Latin hypercube
design} --- a sampling strategy that spreads cases evenly across the
space without exhaustively enumerating it. This gives a broad and
balanced picture of how the ingredients interact, rather than a narrow
snapshot of a few hand-picked scenarios.

Throughout, \textbf{plurality voting serves as the baseline}. Every
other system and pipeline is compared against what plurality would have
produced in the same political environment. That makes the comparisons
concrete: not ``is this system good in the abstract?'' but ``does this
system do better or worse than the most familiar alternative, given the
same voters, the same candidates, and the same strategic pressures?''

The welfare metrics used are simple: how far is the election winner from
the average voter, from the average majority voter, and from the average
minority voter? Smaller distances mean better outcomes. The gap between
the majority and minority scores is the central object of interest
throughout.

\subsection{Preliminary refresher}\label{preliminary-refresher}

The notebook uses the same spatial interpretation as the rest of the
repo. Voters, candidates, and party anchors all live in the same
two-dimensional map. Closer points mean greater ideological similarity.

There are three layers to keep in mind:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  voters and candidates are attached to parties by ideological
  proximity,
\item
  the primary pipeline may remove candidates before the general
  election,
\item
  and the final electoral system chooses an outcome from the candidates
  who remain.
\end{itemize}

If the final winning position is \(y\) and voter \(i\) is at \(x_i\),
then this notebook defines welfare as negative mean distance to the
winner. For the full electorate,

\[
W_{\mathrm{agg}} = - \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \lVert x_i - y \rVert_2.
\]

The same idea is used for the majority and minority groups separately:

\[
W_{\mathrm{maj}} = - \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{maj}}} \sum_{i \in \mathrm{maj}} \lVert x_i - y \rVert_2,
\]

\[
W_{\mathrm{min}} = - \frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{min}}} \sum_{i \in \mathrm{min}} \lVert x_i - y \rVert_2.
\]

Because welfare is negative distance, values closer to zero are better.
The notebook also tracks distance from the electorate geometric median.
If the winner is \(y\) and the electorate median is \(m\), then

\[
d_{\mathrm{median}} = \lVert y - m \rVert_2.
\]

The main comparison with primaries is a matched no-primary baseline. Let
\(Z\) collect all the other ingredients in a sampled case: electorate
family, turnout profile, strategy profile, majority share, candidate
slate, and the intensity parameters. For any metric \(M\), system \(s\),
and primary pipeline \(p\), define

\[
\Delta_M(s, p) = \mathbb{E}_{Z}\left[M(s, p, Z) - M(s, \text{No primary}, Z)\right].
\]

So the object of interest is not just the raw performance of a method
with a primary. It is the average change caused by introducing that
primary, after marginalizing over the rest of the sampled political
setting. In the notebook, this expectation is estimated by averaging the
matched within-case differences over the sampled design.

Because welfare is defined as negative distance, the signs of the
welfare deltas are easy to read. A positive \(\Delta_M(s, p)\) means the
primary pipeline improved that metric relative to the matched no-primary
baseline. A negative value means the pipeline made it worse. Later, when
systems are compared directly to plurality, the notebook uses the same
kind of matched-within-case comparison again, but with plurality as the
reference point instead of no primary.

Plurality stays throughout as the baseline.

Some systems are left out on purpose. \texttt{Party-list\ PR},
\texttt{MMP}, and the \texttt{Fractional} system do not line up cleanly
with this notebook's structure. This notebook is about
candidate-centered primary pipelines feeding into a general election,
with family-matched strategic voting models. Legislative seat-allocation
systems and fractional-outcome systems would need a different
experimental design, so including them here would blur the comparison
rather than strengthen it.

\subsection{The sampled design}\label{the-sampled-design}

The sampled cases vary the same core ingredients as the full conceptual
space, but they do it with a mixed Latin hypercube instead of a
brute-force grid. The continuous pieces are:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  majority share,
\item
  turnout intensity,
\item
  strategy intensity.
\end{itemize}

The sampled categorical pieces are:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  electorate family,
\item
  primary pipeline,
\item
  turnout profile,
\item
  strategy profile,
\item
  candidate slate.
\end{itemize}

A Latin hypercube design starts from the unit cube. If there are \(d\)
continuous variables and the notebook wants \(N\) sampled cases, then
each axis is divided into \(N\) equally sized intervals. For continuous
coordinate \(j\), those intervals are

\[
I_{k,j} = \left[\frac{k-1}{N}, \frac{k}{N}\right), \qquad k = 1, \dots, N.
\]

The design then chooses one sampled point from each interval on each
axis, and permutes those interval choices across dimensions. That gives
a matrix of sampled points

\[
u_i = (u_{i1}, \dots, u_{id}) \in [0,1]^d, \qquad i = 1, \dots, N,
\]

with the key property that every marginal coordinate covers the full
range instead of bunching up in just one corner. In plain language, each
continuous variable gets spread out deliberately, while the joint design
still mixes those variables together.

This notebook uses a mixed version of that idea. The three continuous
coordinates are mapped from \([0,1]\) into meaningful parameter ranges
for majority share, turnout intensity, and strategy intensity. Separate
sampled coordinates are also binned into named categories for electorate
family, primary pipeline, turnout profile, strategy profile, and
candidate slate. Each sampled case is then evaluated under all five
electoral systems, so plurality can still serve as a within-case
baseline.

This is useful because the full cross-product would explode quickly,
while simple random sampling can leave large holes. A mixed Latin
hypercube is a middle ground: it does not evaluate every possible
combination, but it covers the space much more evenly than a naive
random draw.

\subsection{How regressions like this are used in social
science}\label{how-regressions-like-this-are-used-in-social-science}

The summary tables above are good for broad comparisons, but they do not
tell us which ingredients matter most once several political forces move
together. In social science, a common next step is to fit a regression
model that summarizes average associations while adjusting for the other
observed variables in the dataset.

A first instinct is often to remove one block at a time and see how much
\(R^2\) falls. But that becomes hard to interpret when the regressors
overlap heavily. In this notebook, the interaction terms are
deliberately built from the main effects, so blocks like
\texttt{primary\ ×\ system} inevitably share explanatory power with
\texttt{primary} and \texttt{system} on their own. That means a simple
leave-one-block-out drop can make some terms look artificially
unimportant just because nearby terms are soaking up the same variance.

To handle that more carefully, the notebook now uses a
\textbf{Shapley-style \(R^2\) decomposition}. For a term family \(g\),
its contribution is averaged over many possible orderings of the model
blocks:

\[
\phi_g = \frac{1}{|\Pi|} \sum_{\pi \in \Pi} \left[ R^2\bigl(S_{\pi}(g) \cup \{g\}\bigr) - R^2\bigl(S_{\pi}(g)\bigr) \right],
\]

where \(S_{\pi}(g)\) is the set of term families that appear before
\(g\) in ordering \(\pi\). Intuitively, this asks: across many different
ways of building the model up piece by piece, how much extra fit does
this block contribute on average?

The notebook still uses a descriptive linear model rather than a causal
one:

\[
Y_i = \alpha + \sum_g X_{ig} \beta_g + \sum_h Z_{ih} \gamma_h + \varepsilon_i,
\]

where the \(X\) terms are main effects and transformed continuous
covariates, and the \(Z\) terms are selected interaction blocks. The
continuous quantities are treated more flexibly than before:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  minority share,
\item
  turnout intensity,
\item
  strategy intensity.
\end{itemize}

Each one is centered, scaled, and entered both linearly and
quadratically. So the model is allowed to represent curved relationships
instead of forcing everything onto a straight line.

The notebook looks at four outcomes:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  overall welfare, \(W_{\mathrm{agg}}\),
\item
  majority welfare, \(W_{\mathrm{maj}}\),
\item
  minority welfare, \(W_{\mathrm{min}}\),
\item
  the normalized minority-minus-majority welfare gap,
\end{itemize}

\[
\frac{W_{\mathrm{min}} - W_{\mathrm{maj}}}{|W_{\mathrm{agg}}|}.
\]

Using \(|W_{\mathrm{agg}}|\) in the denominator keeps the gap on a
comparable scale across sampled cases, so a given vertical change means
roughly the same thing in a high-welfare case and a low-welfare case.

Computing exact Shapley values for every subset of the full term list
would be expensive, so the notebook uses a permutation approximation: it
samples many orderings, averages the marginal contributions, and then
normalizes them within each outcome:

\[
S_g = \frac{\phi_g}{R^2_{\mathrm{full}}}.
\]

So the heatmap below should be read as a relative decomposition of the
explained signal for each outcome. Larger values mean that block
accounts for more of the fitted \(R^2\) once overlap between correlated
blocks is averaged over more fairly.

minority
shrinks}\label{how-the-normalized-minority-majority-gap-changes-as-the-minority-shrinks}

The regression summary is only one way to look at the data. Because
minority share is one of the continuous dimensions in the Latin
hypercube, it is also useful to look at it directly.

The next figure goes back to a line-style view. Minority share is binned
into evenly populated groups, and each point shows the average
normalized gap inside one bin for one electoral system. The shaded band
shows the middle half of the sampled cases in that bin, so the figure
still gives some sense of spread while making the overall trend easier
to read.

The y-axis uses a normalized minority-minus-majority welfare gap,

\[
\frac{W_{\mathrm{min}} - W_{\mathrm{maj}}}{|W_{\mathrm{agg}}|}.
\]

Values near zero mean the two groups are doing similarly. More negative
values mean the minority is doing worse than the majority, relative to
the overall scale of welfare in that sampled case.

    \subsection{Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

This notebook set out to ask a simple question: when several things
change at once --- who the voters are, how parties narrow the field, who
shows up, and how strategically people vote --- which of those things
actually drives whether elections serve large and small groups well?

The answer that comes back from 1,000 sampled political environments is
fairly consistent, and somewhat surprising.

\textbf{The voting rule itself matters much less than expected.} Once
the simulation accounts for the electorate's structure and the primary
pipeline in front of it, knowing whether the final election uses
plurality, IRV, approval, score, or Condorcet explains almost nothing
about who ends up better or worse off. The Shapley decomposition makes
this concrete: the electoral system label contributes essentially zero
additional explanatory power once upstream factors are held constant.
That does not mean voting rules are unimportant in every context --- but
it does suggest that debates focused narrowly on the counting method may
be missing the larger story.

\textbf{The primary pipeline is where much of the damage happens.}
Relative to a matched no-primary baseline, almost every primary format
reduces welfare on average --- and minority welfare falls roughly twice
as much as overall welfare under the closed pipelines. The exception is
Top-4 open, which stays close to neutral on aggregate welfare and is the
only pipeline in this notebook that occasionally improves minority
welfare on average, particularly when paired with Condorcet or IRV in
the general election. Broadening who can reach the general election
ballot appears to matter more for minority voters than changing how the
final round is counted.

\textbf{The electorate's structure is the dominant factor.} Across all
four welfare outcomes, the combination of electorate family and how the
primary interacts with it accounts for most of the explainable variation
in the results. The practical implication is uncomfortable: if a
society's voters are already sorted into spatially separated groups,
even well-designed electoral institutions can only do so much.
Institutional design operates on top of a social geography it does not
control.

\textbf{The minority-majority welfare gap is persistent but not
monotone.} The normalized gap is negative across the full range of
minority shares examined --- meaning the minority group consistently
ends up farther from the winner than the majority does. But the gap
narrows as the minority grows, and it looks roughly similar across all
five electoral systems. That last point reinforces the voting-rule
finding: at the level of the welfare gap between groups, no system in
this comparison stands out as clearly more protective of minorities than
the others.

The clearest practical takeaway is also the most transferable one: if
the goal is to improve outcomes for minority groups, the design of the
nomination process deserves at least as much attention as the design of
the general election.
